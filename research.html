Research Overview

My research journey has been centered around exploring the deep connections between Algebraic Geometry and modern cryptographic systems. This interdisciplinary approach has allowed me to apply geometric principles to enhance security protocols and develop new perspectives on cryptographic challenges.

During my undergraduate studies, I undertook an independent study of Algebraic Geometry under the guidance of Dr. Sohail Iqbal. This exploration revealed the profound connections between geometric structures and algebraic equations, providing me with powerful tools to analyze and improve cryptographic systems.

My Final Year Project, "Elliptic Curve Cryptography," represents a practical application of these mathematical concepts, where I investigated the security advantages and computational efficiency of elliptic curves in cryptographic protocols.

Currently, I continue to deepen my understanding of Algebraic Geometry, focusing on its theoretical foundations while maintaining connections to applicable domains where geometric insights can solve complex computational problems in cryptography and beyond.

Final Year Project

Elliptic Curve Cryptography

Final Year Project - Bachelors in Mathematics

This project explores the mathematical foundations and practical implementations of Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC), a public-key cryptography approach that offers stronger security per bit compared to traditional cryptographic systems.

The research investigates the algebraic structure of elliptic curves over finite fields and their application in creating efficient cryptographic protocols. Key areas of focus include:

  • Mathematical properties of elliptic curves and their group structure
  • Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem (ECDLP) and its security implications
  • Implementation of ECC for key exchange and digital signatures
  • Comparative analysis with DLP and other cryptographic systems
  • Computational efficiency and security advantages of ECC

This work demonstrates how abstract mathematical concepts from algebraic geometry and number theory can be applied to create practical, secure communication systems.
